The Reliability Doctrine

Most engineers chase what AI can do. Prathik Ravichandran builds for what it must not get wrong. At Medical Metrics, Inc. — a Houston clinical-trial imaging firm serving pharmaceutical and biotech studies worldwide — his agents operate where an unaudited answer isn't a bug. It's a federal incident.

His systems connect an on-premises LLM to clinical databases through MCP servers, answer with citations or not at all, and pass every consequential action through human-in-the-loop gates. Append-only audit logs. Least-privilege tools. He reports, tellingly, to both the CTO and the Chief Compliance Officer.

The doctrine was forged earlier: at Cognizant, building enterprise RAG for financial services when "generative AI" still drew blank stares; at EasyStones in Dallas, where he architected a five-agent LangGraph system — sales, inventory, pricing, marketing, cashflow — negotiating over A2A protocols like a tiny, tireless company.

But the clearest expression is Pathways, his social-impact navigator for Texans leaving incarceration. Housing, employment, legal rights, benefits — delivered conversationally, over web and SMS, to people the technology industry rarely builds for. Seventy-three tests. Seventy-three passing.

He is also the rare engineer who teaches as he ships: at MMI he leads an organization-wide Claude Code rollout, packaging department-scoped Skills as plugins and pairing one-on-one with non-technical staff — 65 people, from clinical reviewers to operations, working alongside agents he built.

"Reliability beats capability. A brilliant agent you can't trust is a liability with good marketing."

— The subject, on first principles
